Section 1 - Statistical Test¶

Q1.1 Which statistical test did you use to analyze the NYC subway data? Did you use a one-tail or a two-tail P value? What is the null hypothesis? What is your p-critical value?

A1.1 In problem set 3, I used a 2-tailed Mann-Whitney U test. The null hypothesis is that the mean number of entries on rainy and non-rainy days are equal. The p-critical value used to determine statistical significance was 5%.

Q1.2 Why is this statistical test applicable to the dataset? In particular, consider the assumptions that the test is making about the distribution of ridership in the two samples.

A1.2 Subway ridership is not normally distributed. A nonparametric test like the Mann-Whitney U test is appropriate regardless of the distribution of the data samples. The Mann-Whitney U test does assume independence of observations.

Q1.3 What results did you get from this statistical test? These should include the following numerical values: p-values, as well as the means for each of the two samples under test.

A1.3 The means of the number of entries on rainy and non-rainy days were 1105.45 and 1090.28 respectively. A MannWhitney test (U = 1924409167, P <= 0.05 two-tailed) indicates that the means of the two distributions (number of entries with and without rain) are statistically different.

Q1.4 What is the significance and interpretation of these results?

A1.4 The test here shows that subway ridership is statistically significantly higher on rainy days compared to non-rainy days at the 5% level.

Q2.1 What approach did you use to compute the coefficients theta and produce prediction for ENTRIESn_hourly in your regression model?

A2.1 In the solution to problem set 3, I used the OLS capability of the statsmodels package to perform regression to predict ENTRIESn_hourly.

Q2.2 What features (input variables) did you use in your model? Did you use any dummy variables as part of your features?

A2.2 The features I used were:

  • Whether it was raining that day ("rain")
  • Whether the day for which the entries are to be predicted is a weekday or weekend (counting 30 May 2011, Memorial Day as a weekend). Weekdays are coded as 0 while non-work days are codes as 1. ("isHoliday")
  • The amount of precipitation that day ("precipi")

  • The mean temperature that day ("meantempi")

    I also used the following dummy features/categorical variables:

  • The time of day for which we are predicting ENTRIESn_hourly ("Hour")
  • The station for which the entries are to be predicted ("UNIT")

Note that since they are binary, the "rain" and "Weekday" features are essentially a categorical variables even though I did not treat them as such in my code. If I had called pandas.get_dummies on these features, I would have gotten 4 new indicator variables. Two of these would have been redundant.

Q2.3 Why did you select these features in your model? We are looking for specific reasons that lead you to believe that the selected features will contribute to the predictive power of your model. Your reasons might be based on intuition. For example, response for fog might be: I decided to use fog because I thought that when it is very foggy outside people might decide to use the subway more often. Your reasons might also be based on data exploration and experimentation, for example: I used feature X because as soon as I included it in my model, it drastically improved my R2 value.

A2.3 From intuition, I realised that the number of entries is a function of time of day (more riders in morning rush hour, for example), the subway station (busier stations have more riders) and the day of week (ridership patterns should change with whether it is a work day or not). I therefore ensured that these variables were accounted for in my model. For the question of day of week, I took care to split distinguish 2 types of days - work days (Mondays - Fridays) and non-work days (Saturdays, Sundays and Memorial Day, which is the last Monday of May). I only used 2 categories to distinguish days so as to reduce the number of variables in the model.

I added "rain" because the purpose of this project is to determine if rain has an impact on ridership, so I wanted to estimate its impact. I expected higher ridership on rainy days as commuters might choose to use the subway rather than walk in the rain. Similarly, "precipi" was added to the model as the amount of rain might influence commuters' decision to take the subway (ie. I would be more likely to take the subway if there was heavy rain than if it was a light drizzle).

Finally, I thought that temperature might be another reason for commuters to choose to take the subway as a commuter might prefer to ride rather than walk on a hot day.

For example, changes in ridership should be a function of the base level of ridership at a station and for a time of day/week. In the linear model used here, an increase in the precipi variable can cause the ridership at a station which typically sees 50 entries/hour to increase to 60 entries/hour. I would not expect that a station which usually has 500 entries/hour to see a similar 10 entries/hour increase to 510 entries/hour. Instead, I would expect a proportional change to 600 entries/hour. In short, the linear model does not account for the scale of the base on which the effect is taking place. It assumes that changes in ENTRIESn_hourly due to each variable are indepdendent of other variables, which is counter-intuitive, as the impact on a busy station should be larger in the absolute number of entries per hour than for a station that is not heavily trafficked.

def linear_regression(features, values):
    """
    Perform linear regression given a data set with an arbitrary number of features.

Q4.1 From your analysis and interpretation of the data, do more people ride the NYC subway when it is raining or when it is not raining?

A4.1 A simple statistical analysis, as in Section 1 above, and the first plot in Section 3 indicates that subway ridership is statistically significantly higher on days when it is raining. I therefore conclude that this dataset provides is no statistical evidence that, all else being equal, subway ridership increases when it is raining.

Q4.2 What analyses lead you to this conclusion? You should use results from both your statistical tests and your linear regression to support your analysis.

Section 5 - Reflection¶

5.1 Please discuss potential shortcomings of the methods of your analysis, including: Dataset; Analysis, such as the linear regression model or statistical test.

A5.1 I believe that the dataset is too small, leading to sampling bias in the analysis. The occurence of rain should be indepdendent of whether a day is a work day or a holiday. However, by only using 1 month worth of days, there is small sample bias, leading to a statistically significant increase in the probability of rain on a non-holiday, as can be seen in the Mann-Whitney U-test result in section 4 immediately above.

As mentioned in Section 2 earlier, the linear model fitted in this project may be inappropriate as well, since the linear model assumes that changes in factors lead to the same changes in ENTRIESn_hourly regardless of the time of day or station. Since some stations have much more traffic than others, as can be seen in plot 2 of Section 3 above, it is reasonable to assume that changes might be proportional to the average entries/hour for each station, rather than being independent of the station.

Finally, the statistical test in Section 1 was limited as it could not control for other effects (for example whether the day is a holiday or not) an thus lead to the erroneous conclusion that there ridership increases on rainy days.

Q5.2 (Optional) Do you have any other insight about the dataset that you would like to share with us?

A5.2 My main insight on the dataset is that its limited size introduces bias into the analysis. I would use data from the month of May (thus controlling for average temperatures, and removing the possibility of snowy days) over several years. By using several years of data, we may be able to add enough samples so that the probability of rain on non-holidays and holidays becomes roughly equal (ie: the expected indepdendence of rain and holidays is exhibited in the dataset). This would increase the size of the dataset, but we can limit the increase by limiting the analysis to just the top 50-100 stations (as seen in Section 3 above, the top 100 stations account for more than 50% of ridership).
